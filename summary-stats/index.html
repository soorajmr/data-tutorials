Mean (Average)

What is Mean?

Often called the average - add all numbers and divide by count. Useful for planning total resources needed and calculating overall capacity requirements.

When to use: When you need to calculate expected totals, plan capacity, or distribute resources.

Important note: Can be misleading when there are extreme values - median may be a better alternative in those situations.

Example: Daily Water Consumption Planning

Daily water consumption (liters) per household across one week (Monday to Sunday):

Mon 185L

Thu 200L
Fri 180L

Average consumption: 204.3 liters per day

The mean value estimated can help in water supply planning - helps calculate total weekly demand (1,430L) and required supply capacity (204L Ã— number of households daily).

Median (Middle Value)

What is Median?

The middle value when numbers are arranged from smallest to largest. Shows what's truly typical when there are extreme values.

When to use: When extreme values would make average misleading - like income, property prices, or waiting times.

Mode (Most Frequent)

What is Mode?

The value that appears most frequently. Shows the most popular choice or most common situation.

When to use: When you want to know what's most typical or popular - for inventory, service planning.

Example: Preferred Transport Option

Transport preferences among commuters:

Bus Bus Auto Bus Metro Bus Auto Bus Bike Bus Metro Bus

Mode: Bus (appears 7 times - most popular transport choice)

Standard Deviation (Spread)

What is Standard Deviation?

Measures how spread out or consistent your data is. Shows predictability and risk - low means consistent, high means variable/unpredictable.

When to use: When consistency matters - quality control, risk assessment, resource planning.

Example: Air Quality Risk Assessment

Daily Air Quality Index readings across one week (Monday to Sunday):

Mon 65 AQI
Tue 85 AQI
Wed 120 AQI
Thu 95 AQI
Fri 110 AQI
Sat 75 AQI
Sun 80 AQI

Mean AQI: 90.0

Standard Deviation: 19.2

Why this matters: Standard deviation shows consistency of air quality across the week - high variation means unpredictable health risks requiring different policy interventions.

Low SD = consistent air quality (easier to plan), High SD = highly variable air quality (needs flexible response plans)

Quantiles (Quartiles & Percentiles)

What are Quantiles?

Values that divide data into equal groups (like quartiles = 4 equal parts). Help understand distribution and set benchmarks - like grade boundaries or income brackets.

When to use: When you need to compare performance or set different service levels for different groups.

Example: Educational Assessment

Student exam scores in a competitive test:

45 52 58 63 67 72 75 78 82 85 87 90 92 94 96

Quartiles for Grade Boundaries:

Q1 (25%): 63 marks Q2 (50% - Median): 78 marks Q3 (75%): 87 marks

Educational authorities use quartiles to identify performance levels and set cut-off marks for different grade categories.
