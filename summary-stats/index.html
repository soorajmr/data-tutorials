Mean (Average)

What is Mean?

The average value - add all numbers and divide by count. Used for planning and resource allocation - tells you what to expect on a typical day.

When to use: When you need to plan resources or set expectations based on typical values.

Example: Daily Water Consumption Planning

Daily water consumption (liters) per household in a residential area over 10 days:

180L 220L 195L 210L 185L 200L 175L 225L 190L 205L

Average consumption: 198.5 liters per day

Municipal planners use this average to determine pipeline capacity and daily supply requirements for the entire area.

Median (Middle Value)

What is Median?

The middle value when numbers are arranged from smallest to largest. Shows what's truly typical when there are extreme values.

When to use: When extreme values would make average misleading - like income, property prices, or waiting times.

Mode (Most Frequent)

What is Mode?

The value that appears most frequently. Shows the most popular choice or most common situation.

When to use: When you want to know what's most typical or popular - for inventory, service planning.

Example: Preferred Transport Mode

Transport preferences among office commuters:

Bus Bus Auto Bus Metro Bus Auto Bus Bike Bus Metro Bus

Mode: Bus (appears 7 times - most popular transport mode)

Transport planners use this to prioritize investment - most popular mode needs maximum capacity.

Standard Deviation (Spread)

What is Standard Deviation?

Measures how spread out or consistent your data is. Shows predictability and risk - low means consistent, high means variable/unpredictable.

When to use: When consistency matters - quality control, risk assessment, resource planning.

Example: Air Quality Risk Assessment

Daily Air Quality Index readings over 10 days in a city:

45 AQI 65 AQI 85 AQI 95 AQI 120 AQI 150 AQI 180 AQI 200 AQI 250 AQI 300 AQI

Mean AQI: 149

Standard Deviation: 83.4

Why this matters: High standard deviation shows highly variable air quality, meaning unpredictable health risks requiring flexible response plans rather than fixed policies.

Low SD = consistent air quality (easier to plan), High SD = highly variable (needs adaptive strategies)

Quantiles (Quartiles & Percentiles)

What are Quantiles?

Values that divide data into equal groups. Help understand distribution and set benchmarks - like grade boundaries or income brackets.

When to use: When you need to compare performance or set different service levels for different groups.

Example: Educational Assessment

Student exam scores in a competitive test:

45 52 58 63 67 72 75 78 82 85 87 90 92 94 96

Quartiles for Grade Boundaries:

Q1 (25%): 63 marks Q2 (50% - Median): 78 marks Q3 (75%): 87 marks

Educational authorities use quartiles to identify performance levels and set cut-off marks for different grade categories.
